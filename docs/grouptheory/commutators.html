Commutators

1. Definitions and basic properties

We define the commutator of $[x,y] = x^{-1}y^{-1}xy$. We immediately verify the following properties:

  • $xy = yx[x,y]$
  • $[y,x] = [x,y]^{-1}$
  • $[xy,z] = [x,z]^y[y,z]$
  • $[z,xy] = [z,y][z,x]^y$

For two subgroups $H,K$ the commutator subgroup is $[H,K] = \langle [h,k] \rangle \lhd \langle H, K \rangle$. In particular, we call $G' = [G,G]$ the derived subgroup.

Theorem 1.1:

If $G = AB$ is the product of abelian subgroups, then $G'$ abelian.

We only need to prove that two commutators of the form $[a_1, b_1]$ and $[a_2, b_2]$ commute. For this write \[\begin{aligned} a_2[a_1, b_1]a_2^{-1} &= [a_1, b_3] \\ b_2[a_1, b_1]a_2^{-1} &= [a_4, b_1], \end{aligned}\] where $b_1 a_2^{-1} = a_3 b_3$ and $a_1 b_2^{-1} = b_4 a_4$. Conjugate the first equation by $b_2^{-1}$ and the second by $a_2^{-1}$.

By a straightforward computation one sees that a factor group $G/N$ is abelian if and only if $G' \leq N$, and hence $G'$ is the unique smallest normal subgroup of $G$ with abelian factor group. By similar computations one can also verify the following generalization:

Lemma 1.2:

$HN/N$ and $KN/N$ commute element-wise if and only if $[H,K] \leq N$.

In particular, if $K < H$ are both normal then $H/K \leq Z(G/K)$ if and only if $[H,G] \leq K$.

2. The lower central series and finite p-groups

The lower central series of a group is recursively defined by $\gamma_1(G) = G$ and $\gamma_n(G) = [\gamma_{n-1}(G),G]$. Note that the lower central series is a reversed (and index-shifted) central series. As a basic consequence of Lemma 1.,. .2 one has

Proposition 2.1:

$G$ nilpotent if and only if $\gamma_n(G) = 1$ for some $n$.

Like for the upper central series, the lower central series is minimal in the sense that the nilpotency class of $G$ is the smallest $c$ such that $\gamma_{c+1}(G) = 1$.

Now if $|G| = p^n$ is a $p$-group we clearly have $\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq n$. But we can do better:

Proposition 2.2:

$|G| = p^n$, then $\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq n - 1$.

It suffices to prove that if $G/\gamma_2(G) \leq p$ implies $\gamma_2(G) = 1$. For this, in turn, one only has to notice that $G/\gamma_3(G)$ is abelian.

This bound is sharp: Let $H = \langle x \rangle \cong C_p$, $K = \langle \sigma \rangle \leq \operatorname{Sym}(p)$ with $\sigma = (1 \, 2 \, \cdots \, p)$ and $G = H \wr K$. For the elements $\xi = (x, 1, \cdots, 1) \in H^p$ and $\xi_i = [\xi, \sigma, \cdots, \sigma] \in \gamma_{i+1}(G)$, one easily verifies that $\xi_{p-1} \neq 1$ and hence $\operatorname{cl}(G) = p$.

In the special case $\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq 2$ we have an important arithmetic rule:

Lemma 2.3:

$\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq 2$, then $(a,b)^t = a^t b^t [b,a]^{t \choose 2}$.

Induction on $t$ and many uses of the basic arithmetic properties.

With this little theory alone, one can fully classify the groups of order $p^3$.

2.1. Groups of order $p^3$

Let $|G| = p^3$. The case $G$ abelian is easy: \[ G \cong C_{p^3}, C_{p^2} \times C_p, C_p \times C_p \times C_p. \] Otherwise we have $\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq 2$ and $|G'| = p$.

If $p = 2$ we have the two possibilities \[ G \cong Q_8, D_8. \]

Assume $p \neq 2$.

1. case: $G$ contains an element of order $p^2$

Call it $a$. First, we assume by contradiction that $A = \langle a \rangle \lhd G$ has no complement in $G$: Then all elements $b$ of order $p$ satisfy $b \in \langle a^p \rangle$. Therefore, an element $b \notin A$ we have $\langle b^p \rangle = \langle a^p \rangle$. Then by Lemma 2.,. .3 $(a b^{-1})^p = 1$. Contradiction.

Hence there is a complement $\langle b \rangle$ and $G = A \rtimes \langle b \rangle$ with $|b| = p$. We count all possible homomorphisms $\langle b \rangle \to \operatorname{Aut}(A)$. $\operatorname{Aut}(A)$ is abelian, so its order is $\phi(p^2) = p (p-1)$. Its Sylow $p$-subgroup (unique!) is generated by the map $a \mapsto a^{p+1}$ and so \[ G \cong \langle a,b,c \mid |a|=p^2, |b|=p, a^b = a^{p+1} \rangle. \]

2. case: $G$ has exponent $p$

Choose a maximal subgroup $H$: $H = \langle a \rangle \times \langle b \rangle$ with $|a| = |b| = p$. Again let $c \notin H$, so $G = H \rtimes \langle c \rangle$, and count the homomorphisms. Note that $\operatorname{Aut}(H) \cong \operatorname{GL}(2,p)$ and that all its elements of order $p$ are conjugates of $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ (by Jordan). Hence \[ G \cong \langle a,b,c \mid |a| = |b| = |c| = p, a^c = ab, b^c = b \rangle, \] which, in fact, has exponent $p$ since $p \neq 2$ and by Lemma 2.,. .3.

General exponent

For general exponent $m$ let $g_m(p)$ be the number of groups of order $p^m$. Computing them is an open problem. For example, the following values are known:

  • $g_1(p) = 1$
  • $g_2(p) = 2$
  • $g_3(p) = 5$
  • $g_4(p) = \begin{cases} 14 \quad &\text{if } p = 2 \\ 15 \quad &\text{otherwise} \end{cases}$
  • $g_5(p) = \begin{cases} 51 \quad &\text{if } p = 2 \\ 67 \quad &\text{if } p = 3 \\ 2p + 2(p - 1,3) + (p-1, 4) + 61 \quad &\text{otherwise} \end{cases}$

It is supposed that these numbers are given by polynomials on residue classes:

CONJECTURE Higman's PORC conjecture

For fixed $n$ there exists $N$ and polynomials $f_{n,i}$ ($0 \leq i \leq N-1$) such that $p \equiv i \mod N$ implies $g_n(p) = f_{n,i}(p)$

2.2. Burnside's basis theorem and the Frattini subgroup

For $p$-groups one can describe the Frattini subgroup precisely. This reveals also certain generating properties.

Theorem 2.4:

$G$ finite. We have $\operatorname{Frat}(G) = G' G^p$ and $G/\operatorname{Frat(G)} \cong C_p^d$ such that $d = d(G)$ is the smallest cardinality of a generating set of $G$.

"$\geq$": $G/M = C_p$ for a maximal subgroup $M$.

"$\cong$" and "$\leq$": $G/G'G^p$ is abelian of exponent $p$, hence isomorphic to $C_p^d$ and so $\operatorname{Frat}(G/G'G^p) = 1$.

"smallest": $d \leq d(G)$ clear. Reverse follows from the uselessness of the Frattini subgroup.

As consequence one gets

Theorem 2.5: Burnside's basis theorem

$G$ finite $p$-group with $|G/\operatorname{Frat}(G)| = p^d$. Every set of generators contains a generating subset of $d$ elements.

$G/\operatorname{Frat}(G)$ is a $d$-dimensional vector space over $\Z/p\Z$.

We now prove two important applications as consequences of the previous considerations.

For the first, remember that a finite group with a unique maximal subgroup is cyclic (another consequences of the uselessness of Frattini's subgroup). Now we assume that $G$ has a unique minimal subgroup. By Sylow theorems $G$ must be a $p$-group.

Theorem 2.6:

If $p\neq 2$ and $G$ finite with a unique minimal subgroup, then $G$ is cyclic.

Induction on |G|, so all maximal subgroups of $G$ are cyclic. From this we can deduce that it suffices to prove the case $d \leq 2$. Take $\langle a \rangle \neq \langle b \rangle$ max. subgroups s.t. $G = \langle a,b\rangle$. Then $\operatorname{Frat}(G) = \langle a \rangle \cap \langle b \rangle \leq Z(G)$, and so $\operatorname{cl}(G) \leq 2$.

W.l.o.g. $\operatorname{Frat}(G) = \langle f \rangle \neq 1$ and $a^p = b^p = f$. Now if $|f| = p^m$, say, then we must have $|g| = p^{m+1}$ for all $g \notin \langle f \rangle$. Hence for $c = ab^{-1}$ one proves that $c^p \in \langle f^p \rangle$, and, consequently, $c \in \operatorname{Frat}(G)$. It follows $\langle a,b \rangle = \langle a \rangle$.

And, finally, a bound for the automorphism group of a $p$-group.

Theorem 2.7:

$|G| = p^n$ with $|G/\operatorname{Frat}(G)| = p^d$. Then $\operatorname{Aut}(G) \mid p^{(n-d)d} t$ where $t = |\operatorname{GL}(d,p)|$.

As the Frattini subgroup $F$ is characteristic, the following is a well-defined homomorphism \[ \gamma \colon \operatorname{Aut}(G) \to \operatorname{Aut}(G/F), \alpha \mapsto (gF \mapsto g^\alpha F) \] with kernel $C$. After an application of the homomorphism theorem we see that it suffices to prove that $|C|$ divides $p^{(n-d)d}$.

Component-wise conjugation defines an action of $C$ on $\Omega = \{(g_1 f_1, \cdots, g_d f_d)\}$, where $G = \langle g_1, \cdots, g_d \rangle$ is a fixed generating set. Note that the stabilizers are $1$ and conclude.

3. The derived series and the three-subgroups lemma

We define the derived series recursively by $G^{(0)} = G$ and $G^{(n)} = (G^{(n-1)})'$. Similar to the lower central series this is a reversed abelian series:

Proposition 3.1:

$G$ soluble if and only if $G^{(n)} = 1$ for some $n$.

It is, moreover, minimal in the sense that the minimal $n$ such that $G^{(n)} = 1$ is the derived length of $G$.

3.1. Interlude: Burnside's problem

Question: Are finitely generated torsion groups finite?

The answer is clearly positive, if $G$ is abelian.

It is also positive if $G$ is nilpotent: To see this we define the abelianization $G_{ab} = \gamma_1(G) / \gamma_2(G)$. The map \[\begin{aligned} \gamma_i(G)/\gamma_{i+1}(G) \times G_{ab} &\to \gamma_{i+1}(G)/\gamma_{i+2}(G) \\ (a \gamma_{i+1}(G), g \gamma_2(G)) &\mapsto [a,g] \gamma_{i+2}(G) \end{aligned}\] is a well-defined, surjective, bilinear map. Therefore, by tensoring one sees that $\gamma_{i+1}(G) / \gamma_{i+2}(G)$ is an epimorphic image of $\gamma_i(G) / \gamma_{i+1}(G) \otimes G_{ab}$. Iterating yields an epimorphism $G_{ab} \otimes \cdots \otimes G_{ab} \to \gamma_{i}(G) / \gamma_{i+1}(G)$ of $i$ copies of $G_{ab}$.

Theorem 3.2:

$\mathcal{P}$ group-theoretical property, inherited by images of tensor products and by extensions. If $G$ is nilpotent and $G_{ab}$ has $\mathcal{P}$, then $G$ has $\mathcal{P}$.

Write the lower central series from big to low, traverse from top to bottom to see that all the quotients have $\mathcal{P}$ and then all the way back to verify that by iterated extensions also $G$ has $\mathcal{P}$.

For example, this holds for finite groups, torsion groups, $\pi$-groups and finitely generated groups.

Therefore, if $G$ is finitely generated and torsion, so is $G_{ab}$ as a quotient and hence $G_{ab}$ is finite as it is abelian. Therefore, $G$ is finite.

The answer is also "yes" for soluble groups.

Lemma 3.3:

$|G:H| < \infty$, $G$ finitely generated $\implies$ $H$ finitely generated.

Fix a finite set of generators $X$ of $G$, such that $X = X^{-1}$, and a right transversal $T$ of $H$ containing the $1$. Note that for every $t$ and $x$ there exists a unique $\overline{tx} \in T$ such that $Htx = H\overline{tx}$ and note that $Y = \{ tx(\overline{tx})^{-1} \}$ is a finite subset of $H$. Then by induction one can easily prove that any $g = x_1 \cdots x_r$ can be rewritten as $g = y_1 \cdots y_r t$.

So if $G$ is soluble, torsion and finitely generated one can deduce finiteness by induction on the derived length of $G$, since Lemma 3.,. .3 implies that in this case $G/G'$ and $G'$ are finite.

To see that finite-generatedness is needed consider the following example:

Let $G = A \rtimes B$, where $A = C_{2^\infty}$ and $B = \la b \ra = C_2$. Note that $G$ is a $2$-group and since $G/A = B$ we have $G' \leq A$. One deduces that $G' = [A,B] = A$ and hence $G/G'$ is finite but $G$ is not while $G$ is soluble of derived length $2$.

Generally, though, the answer is negative. In 1976 Adian and Novikov proved that the Burnside group $B(m,n)$ (the free group with $m$ generators and exponent $n$) is, in fact, infinite for $m \geq 2$ and $n \geq 665$.

We know that, if it is, the group has order $5^{34}$.

3.2. The three-subgroups lemma

We use left-association to denote multi-commutators. By direct computation:

Lemma 3.4: Hall-Witt identity

\[ [x, y^{-1}, z]^y [y, z^{-1}, x]^z [z, x^{-1}, y]^x = 1 \]

Proposition 3.5: Three-subgroups lemma

If two of the three subgroups $[H,K,L]$, $[K,L,H]$, $[L,H,K]$ are contained in a normal subgroup $N$ then so is the third.

With this we can establish relations between the lower central series and the derived series.

Theorem 3.6:
  1. $[\gamma_i(G), \gamma_j(G)] \leq \gamma_{i+j}(G)$
  2. $G^{(r)} \leq \gamma_{2^r}(G)$
  3. If $G$ is nilpotent with nilpotency class $c$ and derived length $d$, then \[ d \leq 1 + \log_2 c \]

"(1)": By induction on $j$ and with Proposition 3.,. .5.

"(3)": derived from $1 = \gamma_{c+1}(G) \leq G^{(d-1)} \leq \gamma_{2^{d-1}}$.

Finally, we can prove a solubility criterion:

Theorem 3.7:

Suppose that $A$ acts via automorphism on $G$ and assume $[G,A, \dots, A] = 1$ of $m$ copies of $A$. Then $A^{(m-1)} \leq C_A(G)$.

Let $H = [G,A]$. By induction on $m$ since $[H, A, \cdots, A]$ with $m-1$ copies we have $A^{(m-2)} \leq C_A(H)$. Note that $A \supset A^{(m-2)}$ and \[ 1 = [H,A^{(m-2)}] = [G,A,A^{(m-2)}] = [G,A^{(m-2)},A^{(m-2)}] = [A^{(m-2)}, G, A^{(m-2)}]. \] Conclude by applying Proposition 3.,. .5

In particular, if the action is faithful then $C_A(G) = 1$ (since it is the kernel of the action) and $A$ is soluble of derived length $m-1$.
