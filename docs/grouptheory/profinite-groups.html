Profinite Groups

1. Topological groups

A topological group is a group endowed with a topology such that the group operations are continuous. We quickly summarize important properties without proof:

Theorem 1.1:

$G$ topological group. $H \leq G$. $X,Y \subseteq G$.

  1. $X$ open or closed, then so are $gX$, $Xg$ and $X^{-1}$.
  2. $\overline{H} \leq G$.
  3. $C_G(X)$ is closed.
  4. $N_G(H)$ is closed.
  5. $H$ open, then $H$ closed.
  6. $H$ closed and $[G:H]$ finite, then $H$ open.
  7. $H$ open and $G$ compact, then $[G:H]$ finite.
  8. $H$ normal, then the projections $G \to G/H$ are open.
  9. $G$ Hausdorff if and only if $\{1\}$ is closed.
  10. $K$ normal, then $G/K$ is Hausdorff if and only if $K$ is closed.
  11. $G$ totally disconnected, then $G$ Hausdorff.
  12. $G$ compact and $X, Y$ closed, then $XY$ closed.
  13. $X$ clopen with $1 \in X$, then $X$ contains a normal open subgroup.
  14. $G$ compact and totally disconnected, then any open subset of $G$ is a union of cosets of open normal subgroups.
  15. $G$ compact and totally disconnected, then \[ \overline{X} = \bigcap \{ NX \mid N \lhd_o G \}. \]
  16. $G$ compact and totally disconnected, then $G$ residually finite.
  17. $G$ compact and totally disconnected. $G$ infinite, then $G$ not countable.

2. Inverse limits

A directed set is a poset $I$ such that any two $a, b \in I$ are bounded by some $c \in I$, i.e. $a \leq c$ and $b \leq c$.

An inverse system $\{X_i, \varphi_{ij}\}$ is a family (indexed by a directed set) of sets $\{X_i\}_{i \in I}$ together with a family of maps $\{ \varphi_{ij} \colon X_j \to X_i \mid i,j \in I \text{ and } i \leq j\}$ $\varphi_{ij} \colon $ such that $\varphi_{ii} = \operatorname{id}_{X_i}$ and $\varphi_{ij} \circ \varphi_{jk} = \varphi_{ik}$ whenever $i \leq j \leq k$.

Given an inverse system we call a family of maps $\{ \varphi_i \colon Y \to X_i \}$ a compatible family on $Y$ if $\varphi_i = \varphi_{ij} \circ \varphi_j$ whenever $i \leq j$.

Now an inverse limit $X$ of such an inverse system is a set $X$ together with a compatible family $\{ \pi_i \}_i$ on $X$ such that the following universal property holds

For every other compatible family $\{ \varphi_i \}$ on a space $Y$ there exists a unique map $\sigma \colon Y \to X$ such that $\varphi_i = \pi_i \circ \sigma$.

Proposition 2.1:

Inverse limits exist and are unique.

Uniqueness is routine and the construction proceeds as follows:

Let $C = \prod_i X_i$ be the direct product and $\pi_i$ the projections. Then we define \[ X = \left\{ c \in C \mid (\varphi_{ij} \circ \pi_j)(c) = \pi_i(c) \text{ for all } i \leq j \right\} \] and let $\varphi_i = \pi_i|_X$.

Hence we may use the notation $\lim^{\leftarrow} X_i$ unambiguously. Note that we may exchange the category of sets by the category of topological spaces or groups, and still get a topological space or a group.

2.1. Category of topological spaces

Without proof we state the following properties when working in the category of topological spaces:

Theorem 2.2:

Let $X = \lim^{\leftarrow} X_i$ be the inverse limit of topological spaces.

  1. If the $X_i$ are totally disconnected, so is $X$.
  2. If the $X_i$ are Hausdorff, so is $X$.
  3. If the $X_i$ are Hausdorff, then $X$ is closed in $\prod_i X_i$.
  4. If the $X_i$ are Hausdorff, then the subsets $\varphi_i^{-1}(U)$, where $U$ is open in $X_i$, form a basis of the topology of $X$.
  5. If the $X_i$ are Hausdorff and compact, then $X$ is compact.
  6. If the $X_i$ are not empty, Hausdorff and compact, then $X$ is not empty.
  7. If the $G_i$ are non-empty, Hausdorff and compact, then for all $i$ \[\varphi_i(X) = \bigcap_{j \geq i} \varphi_{ij}(X_j). \]
  8. If $Y \subseteq X$ is such that $\varphi_i(Y) = X_i$ for all $i$, then $Y$ is dense.
  9. If $Y$ is a space and $\alpha \colon Y \to X$, then $\alpha$ is continuous if and only if all the $\varphi_i \circ \alpha$ are continuous.

2.2. Category of topological groups

Now we turn to topological groups (and finally start proving things). Throughout, let $G = \lim^{\leftarrow} G_i$ be the inverse limit of topological groups.

Lemma 2.3:

If all the $G_i$ Hausdorff and $L \lhd_o G$, then there exists $i$ s.t. $\ker(\varphi_i) \subseteq L$.

Since $L$ is open, by Theorem 2.,. .2 it can be written as the union of inverse images of a compatible map of an open subgroup of some $G_i$. Choose $i$ and $U \subseteq G_i$ such that $1 \in \varphi_i^{-1}(U) \subseteq L$.

We call a family $I$ of subgroups of $G$ a filtering basis if for any $K_1, K_2 \in I$ there is a third $K_3$ such that $K_3 \subseteq K_1 \cap K_2$.

Theorem 2.4:

$G$ topological group. $I$ filtering basis of normal closed subgroups. Then $\{G/K\}_{K \in I}$ together with the family of natural maps $\{ \varphi_{K_1 K_2} \colon G/K_2 \to G/K_1 \}_{K_1 \geq K_2}$ form an inverse system. Let \[ \hat G = \lim^{\longleftarrow}_{K \in I} G/K \] and $\theta \colon G \to \prod_{K \in I} G/K$ the natural map. Then we have:

  1. $\theta$ is a continuous group homomorphism.
  2. $\theta(G) \leq \hat G$.
  3. $\theta(G)$ is dense in $\hat G$.
  4. $\ker \theta = \bigcap_{K \in I} K$.
  5. If $G$ compact, then $\theta(G) = \hat G$.
  6. If $G$ compact and $\bigcap_{K \in I} K = 1$, then $G$ and $\hat G$ are isomorphic and homeomorphic.

"(1)": All the projections are continuous + Theorem 2.,. .2 (9)

"(2)": Clear as the maps $\varphi_{K_i K_j}$ are surjective.

"(3)": Immediate by the density criterion Theorem 2.,. .2 (8).

"(5)": Note that $\theta(G)$ is compact in a Hausdorff space, hence closed.

"(6)": Use the fact that a continuous bijection from a compact to a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism.

3. Pro-$\mathcal{C}$ groups and completions

Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a class of finite groups, which is closed under subgroups, epimorphic images and finite cartesian products.

  • A $\mathcal{C}$-group is an elements of this class.
  • A pro $\mathcal{C}$-group is a topological group that can be obtained as an inverse limit of $\mathcal{C}$-groups (each endowed with the discrete topology).
  • In the case where $\mathcal{C}$ is the class of all finite groups we call $G$ a profinite group.
Theorem 3.1:

TFAE:

  1. $G$ is a pro-$\mathcal{C}$-group
  2. $G$ is isomorphic to a closed subgroup of a direct product of $\mathcal{C}$-groups.
  3. $G$ is compact and $\bigcap \{ N \mid N \lhd_o G, G/N \in \mathcal{C} \} = 1$.
  4. $G$ is compact, totally disconnected and $G/N \in \mathcal{C}$ for any $N \lhd_o G$.

"(1) $\implies$ (2)": The finite groups $G_\lambda$ with the discrete topology are Hausdorff. Use Theorem 2.,. .2 (3).

"(2) $\implies$ (3)": Compactness follows from Tychonoff and for the intersection note that the kernels of the compatible maps are closed and of finite index, hence open, and that they have empty intersection.

"(3) $\implies$ (1)": Theorem 2.,. .4

"(1) $\implies$ (4)": Lemma 2.,. .3

"(4) $\implies$ (3)": Theorem 1.,. .1
