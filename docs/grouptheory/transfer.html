Transfer Maps

We develop theory to produce normal subgroups. The main result of this section is a result by Burnside that produces normal p-complements.

1. Definition and simple properties

Let $G$ be a group, $H \leq_f G$ and $T$ a right transversal of $H$ in $G$. Given $g\in G$, $t \in T$ there exists a uniquely determined action $t \circ g \in T$ such that $Htg = H(t \circ g)$. This asserts the well-definedness of the transfer map to $H$: \[ v \colon G \to H/H', g \mapsto \prod_{t \in T} t g (t \circ g)^{-1} H'. \] It is independent of the chosen transversal and a group homomorphism.

To compute the transversal for a fixed $g$ one can consider the orbits $O_1, \cdots, O_r$ produced by the action of $\langle g \rangle$ on $T$. Fix elements $t_1, \cdots, t_r$ inside and write $n_i = |O_{t_i}|$. Then one has $O_i = \{ t_i \circ g^j \mid 0 \leq j < n_i \}$ and $t_i = t_i \circ g^{n_i}$. Therefore, \[\begin{aligned} &\prod_{s \in O_i} s g (s \circ g)^{-1} \\ =\,\, &(t_i g (t_i \circ g)^{-1}) \, ((t_i \circ g) g (t_i \circ g^2)^{-1}) \, \cdots \, ((t_i \circ g^{n_i - 1}) g (t_i \circ g^{n_i})^{-1}) \\ =\,\, &t_i g^{n_t} t_i^{-1}, \end{aligned}\] and hence \[v(g) = \prod_i = t_i g^{n_i} t_i^{-1}. \] We summarize these considerations for reference:

Lemma 1.1:

Fix $g$. There exists $T_0 \subset T$ and $n_t$ for $t \in T_0$ such that:

  • $t g^{n_t} t^{-1} \in H$
  • $ v(g) = \prod_{t \in T_0} t g^{n_t} t^{-1} H' $
  • $\sum_{t \in T_0} n_t = [G:H]$
  • $n_t \mid |g|$

As a simple consequences:

  • If $H \leq Z(G)$ then $v(g) = g^{[G:H]}$.
  • In particular, $Z(G) \leq_f G$ implies $(G')^{[G:H]} = 1$.
Theorem 1.2:

$G$ finite. If $p \mid |G' \cap Z(G)|$, then all Sylow $p$-subgroups are not abelian.

Suppose $P$ is abelian Sylow. By Sylow theorems there is $z \in P \cap G' \cap Z(G)$ since $G' \cap Z(G)$ is normal. As $z$ is central the transfer $v \colon G \to P$ maps $v(z) = z^{[G:P]}$. By isomorphism theorems $G / \ker(v)$ is abelian and hence $G' \leq \ker(v)$. But then $1 = v(z) = z^{[G:P]}$. Contradiction.

In the next sections we discuss further, more complicated applications of transfers.

2. Applications

2.1. Finiteness condition on the derived subgroup

Our goal is to establish conditions for the derived subgroup to be finite. First a simple observation:

If we fix is a right transversal $T$ of $Z(G)$ in $G$, we may write all commutators in $G$ as $[s,t]$ for $s,t \in T$. In particular, $Z(G)$ has finite index, there are only finitely many commutators. This lets us suspect the following:

Theorem 2.1: Schur

$[G:Z(G)] < \infty$ implies $|G'| < \infty$.

As the derived subgroup is the subgroup generated by all commutators, so we need another criterion.

Theorem 2.2: Dietzmann

$G$ group, $X \subset G$ finite and closed under conjugation. If there is a positive integer $n$ such that $X^n = 1$, then $\langle X \rangle$ is finite.

Let $S = \langle X \rangle$. Every $g \in S$ is a finite word in $X$. Assume $g = x_1 \dots x_m$ and fix $x \in X$. One can rewrite $g$ such that $x$ is in front: If $t$ is the first appearance, then \[\begin{aligned} x &= (x_1 \dots x_t)(x_{t+1} \dots x_m) \\ &= x (x_1 \dots x_{t-1})^x (x_{t+1} \cdots x_m) \\ &= x x_1^x \dots x_{t-1}^x x_{t+1} \dots x_m. \end{aligned}\] Note that if $x$ appeared at least $k$ times, this still holds after the rewrite.

Now assume that $m$ is minimal. By assumption and the previous procedure we find that an $x$ can appear at most $(n-1)$ times and so $m \leq (n-1)|X|$.

We can prove Theorem 2.,. .1 now:

Clearly, the set of commutators is closed under conjugation and by previous observations of transfers we have $(G')^{[G:Z(G)]} = 1$. We conclude with Theorem 2.,. .2.

2.2. Producing normal prime complements

Let $G$ be finite. The goal of this section is

Theorem 2.3: Burnside's theorem of normal prime complements

$P \in \operatorname{Syl}_p(G)$. If $P \leq Z(N_G(P))$, then $G$ has a normal $p$-complement.

The proof requires some preparation.

Since we are looking for a $\{p\}'$-Hall subgroup, we first summarize the basic properties of transfers to Hall subgroups:

  • $G = H \ker(v)$ by cardinality arguments.
  • $v(H) = v(G)$.
  • $G / \ker(v) = H / H \cap \ker(v)$ by isomorphism theorem.

If $H \leq G$ is Hall and $v$ the transer to $H$, one sees by cardinality arguments that $G = H \ker(v)$ and hence $v(H) = v(G)$.

Next we need a simple lemma:

Lemma 2.4:

$P \in \operatorname{Syl}_p(G)$, $x,y \in C_G(P)$. If $x$ and $y$ are conjugated in $G$, then they are conjugated also in $N_G(P)$.

Note first that $P \in \operatorname{Syl}_p(C_G(y))$ and, as $y = x^g$, also $P^g \in \operatorname{Syl}_p(C_G(y))$. So there is $c \in C_G(y)$ such that $P = P^{gc}$, which implies $gc \in N_G(P)$. Finally $x^{gc} = y$.

The proof of Theorem 2.,. .3 proceeds as follows:

The previous lemma in this setup implies that no distinct elements of $P$ are conjugates. The transfer $v$ to $P$ hence reduces to $v(x) = x^{[G:P]}$ for $x \in P$ and its restriction to $P$ is injective. Therefore, $P \cap \ker(v) = 1$ and we have found a complement.

Corollary 2.5:

$p$ the smallest prime divisor of $|G|$ and $P$ a Sylow $p$-subgroup. If $P$ cyclic, then $G$ has a normal $p$-complement.

$P \cong C_{p^n}$. Since $N_G(P) / C_G(P) \leq \operatorname{Aut}(P)$, its order divides $\phi(p^n) = p^{n-1}(p-1)$. But $p$ is the smallest prime divisor and $P \leq C_G(P)$, and so the order must be $1$. Hence we may apply Theorem 2.,. .3.

As an immediate consequence: If $G$ is non-abelian group, that has a cyclic Sylow $2$-subgroup, then $G$ is not simple.

Corollary 2.6:

If all Sylow subgroups of $G$ are simple, then $G$ is soluble.

Take the smallest prime divisor $p$. By Theorem 2.,. .3 there is a normal $p$-complement $N$, which, in turn, also has cyclic Sylow subgroups. By induction $N$ is cyclic and $G/N$ is a $p$-group.
